# Builder-Polymath LinkedIn Profile Reference Scan

**Research date:** 2026-02-23
**Purpose:** Reference material for crafting a LinkedIn profile with "builder-polymath" energy — wide-ranging technical skills, many projects shipped, hacker/tinkerer tone. Contrast with corporate/generic defaults.

---

## LinkedIn Character Limits (2025)

| Field | Limit | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Headline | 220 chars (desktop) / 240 chars (mobile) | First thing people see; most critical real estate |
| About section | 2,600 chars | Only ~200-300 chars visible before "See more" click |
| Experience title | 100 chars | |
| Experience description | 2,000 chars | |
| Skills (per skill) | 80 chars | |
| Publication title | 250 chars | |
| Connection message | 300 chars | |
| Posts | 3,000 chars | Only ~210 chars visible before "See more" |

**Key insight:** The "About" section shows roughly the first 200-300 characters before truncation. Your hook must land in those first 2-3 lines or you lose the reader entirely.

- Returned to OpenAI briefly (2023-2024)
- Founded Eureka Labs (2024) — AI education company
- Coined "vibe coding" (February 2025)

### What Makes Him Stop-Scroll

- **That headline** — "I like to train deep neural nets on large datasets" is deceptively simple. Coming from him, it's like Federer saying "I like to hit tennis balls." The understatement is the flex.
- **Teaching as a core identity** — CS231n is one of the most-watched ML course series in the world. He doesn't just build; he explains the internals to everyone. This is a different kind of builder.
- **Deep builder energy in public** — YouTube tutorials, neural net explainers, hands-on implementations. He writes his own code, shows his reasoning, doesn't hide behind abstractions.

Precise, humble, deeply competent. The tone is that of someone so far inside the problem that they have no need to perform expertise — they just describe what they do with technical accuracy and let the work speak. Zero corporate language. The whole profile reads like a smart person's README.

### Experience Framing
Each role described around what was *built or taught*, not around titles or responsibilities. CS231n = "architect and lead instructor." Tesla = "built the vision team, handled data labeling + NN training + deployment." OpenAI = "worked on midtraining and synthetic data."

## What Makes a Profile Feel "Dangerous" vs Corporate vs Generic

### The Corporate Profile (what to avoid)
- Headline: "Experienced Product Leader | Driving Innovation | Passionate About Technology"
- About: "Results-driven professional with 10+ years of experience delivering cross-functional business value..."
- Experience: "Led a team to achieve key business objectives and drive stakeholder alignment"
- Signal: invisible. No one is hiring this person because of this profile. Nothing is memorable.

### The Generic Builder Profile (better but still weak)
- Headline: "Founder & CEO at [Company] | Ex-[BigCo]"
- About: Lists companies founded, exit, current role. Chronological. No story.
- Experience: Job titles, dates, bullet points of responsibilities
- Signal: You've done things but you sound like everyone else who's done things

### The Dangerous Profile (what to aim for)
Characteristics shared across the scanned profiles:

**1. The work IS the about section**
Karpathy's headline isn't "AI Researcher and Educator." It's "I like to train deep neural nets on large datasets." The work itself, stated directly, is more compelling than any claim about the work.

**2. Credentials are a launching pad, not the destination**
Balaji writes "Formerly CTO of Coinbase." The word "formerly" signals he has already moved on to something bigger in his mind. The best profiles use past credentials as context, not trophy cases.

**3. Specific number or fact that creates a story in 5 words**
- "12 startups in 12 months" (Levels)
- "First person to unlock an iPhone" (Hotz)
- "Lead instructor, CS231n" (Karpathy)
- "Second employee at Pinterest, age 19" (Lavingia)
Each of these implies a whole arc. You can't read them without wanting to know more.

**4. Unconventional range signals genuine curiosity**
- Graham: programmer + painter + essayist
- Balaji: electrical engineer + genomicist + crypto CTO + author of political theory
- Lavingia: software founder + writer + oil painter
- Hotz: security hacker + autonomous vehicles + AI infrastructure + rap musician
The range isn't name-dropping; it's evidence of a person who follows genuine interest across domain lines.

**5. Philosophical conviction about an unusual position**
- Hotz: "tinygrad should be <1000 lines" (simplicity over scale as a principle)
- Levels: "one person can build a $1M/year business" (solo over team as philosophy)
- Graham: "Lisp is the right tool" (minority technical opinion held with confidence)
- Lavingia: "no meetings, no deadlines, no full-time employees" (async-first as operating system)
Having a position that most people don't share, and building something from it, is credibility.

**6. Primary signal is shipped product, not claimed expertise**
None of these profiles rely on "I am skilled in X." They rely on "I built Y, you can go use it right now." The link to a live product is more convincing than any adjective.

**7. Anti-LinkedIn energy is fine (if you can back it up)**
Hotz being banned from LinkedIn and treating it as a punchline is on-brand and credible because he has actual hacker credentials. This only works if the body of work is already there. Without it, it's just contrarianism.

## About Section Structure: What Works for Builder Profiles

Based on cross-referencing the scanned profiles with best practices research:

### Opening Hook Formulas (must land in first 200-300 chars)
| Formula | Example |
|---------|---------|
| The unconventional fact | "I sold all my stuff and built 12 startups in 12 months." |
| The specific achievement | "I was the first person to jailbreak an iPhone. I was 17." |
| The direct technical statement | "I like to train deep neural nets on large datasets." |
| The philosophical provocation | "One person can build a $1M/year company without investors, employees, or an office." |
| The failure/pivot | "I almost destroyed Gumroad. Here's what I learned." |

### What to Avoid in the Opener
- "I am a passionate and results-driven..."
- "With over X years of experience..."
- "I help businesses achieve..."
- "Welcome to my profile!"
- Anything that could be the first line of 10,000 other profiles

### Structure for Builder-Polymath About (1,200-1,800 chars recommended)
1. **Hook** (1-2 sentences, 150-200 chars) — the specific, surprising thing
2. **What I build / what I'm working on now** (2-3 sentences) — concrete, present tense
3. **The range / the thread** (2-3 sentences) — how different things connect, what the obsession actually is
4. **Proof / receipts** (bullet list or short sentences) — live products, specific metrics, things you can verify
5. **What's next / current bet** (1-2 sentences) — optional but signals continued forward motion
6. **CTA or contact** (1 sentence) — direct, not corporate

## Featured Section: What Builder-Polymaths Typically Pin

Based on the profiles scanned and best practices research:

**Tier 1 (most compelling):**
- Link to live product you built — click and use it immediately
- Best essay or long-form post (especially if controversial or viral)
- Talk or interview that shows thinking in depth (Lex Fridman, YC talks, etc.)

**Tier 2 (good):**
- Newsletter or writing archive
- GitHub profile or specific open-source project
- Press coverage (but only if genuinely good coverage, not just any mention)

**Avoid:**
- Generic company website homepage
- Generic job postings
- Anything that requires signing up before delivering value
- Too many items (4-6 max, curated, not exhaustive)

## Experience Section: How Builders Frame It

### Corporate framing (weak):
> **Senior Director of Product, TechCorp (2019-2022)**
> Led cross-functional teams to deliver product roadmap initiatives. Drove alignment across stakeholders. Achieved 15% YoY growth.

### Builder framing (strong):
> **Nomad List (2014-present) | solo founder**
> Built the largest community for digital nomads from scratch. Grew to $X MRR, $Y annual revenue. One person, no investors, no employees. Built the entire stack.

The difference:
- Active verbs tied to specific outputs, not processes
- Numbers that are verifiable or at least specific
- Context for *why* the project exists, not just *what* it is
- Ownership is explicit ("I built" vs "Led a team")
