## Consumer Segment

**Who:** Philippine domestic corporations and one-person corporations (OPCs) registered with the SEC that are not entitled to PEZA/BOI/other special incentive regimes and thus face a straightforward RCIT vs. MCIT determination. This includes the finance officer, CFO, or owner-manager who runs the annual corporate income tax computation. Small domestic corporations (net taxable income ≤ ₱5M AND total assets ≤ ₱100M, excluding land) qualify for the reduced 20% CIT rate under CREATE Act (RA 11534, Sec. 27(B)).

**Population:**
- **527,710** total active SEC-registered companies as of end-2024 — Source: SEC press release / BusinessMirror February 2025 — Confidence: OFFICIAL
- **~478,000–480,000** total active SEC-registered companies as of end-2023 — Source: DERIVED: 527,710 − 52,304 new 2024 registrations (SEC 2024 data) — Confidence: DERIVED
  - New OPC registrations in 2024: 8,640 (SEC/BusinessMirror Feb 2025) — Confidence: OFFICIAL
  - New registrations all types in 2023: 49,506 (SEC press release/BusinessMirror) — Confidence: OFFICIAL
- **~406,000–431,000** of active corporations likely qualify as "small" (total assets ≤ ₱100M) and thus eligible for 20% reduced CIT rate — Source: DERIVED using assumption that 85–90% of SEC-registered companies are micro/small/medium enterprises by assets (consistent with PSA MSME stats where 99.6% of PH businesses are MSMEs); no SEC breakdown by asset size publicly available — Confidence: DERIVED
- **4,625,017** total BIR-registered business taxpayers (as of November 2021, all types including individual sole proprietors) — Source: BIR press release November 2021 (cited in web search results) — Confidence: OFFICIAL
  - Note: This larger figure includes individual self-employed/sole proprietors. It is NOT the corporate taxpayer count. The relevant population for A2 is SEC-registered corporations (~478K–480K).

**Addressable fraction:**
- Corporate entities, by nature, have institutional internet access and digital banking
- Use **90%** as addressable fraction (excludes shell companies, dormant registrations, entities that have effectively ceased operations but haven't been formally revoked)

**Addressable consumer population:** 478,000 × 90% = **430,200**

## Professional Segment

**Who:** CPAs and accounting firms that handle corporate income tax returns (BIR Form 1702-RT / 1702-EX / 1702-MX) for multiple corporate clients. A practice tool that processes RCIT/MCIT/MCIT carryforward calculations across a client portfolio rather than one-at-a-time.

**Population:**
- **~200,000** total registered CPAs — Source: BOA Chairman, Manila Times May 2023 — Confidence: ESTIMATED
- **CPAs in public practice** (external audit + tax): Estimated 25–30% of total = **50,000–60,000** — Confidence: ESTIMATED
- **CPAs actively handling corporate tax returns** (1702 filings): Estimated 35–45% of public practice CPAs = **17,500–27,000**. Using **20,000** as working estimate — Confidence: ESTIMATED
  - Each CPA in public practice serves an average of 10–30 corporate clients/year per SEC active corporation demand. With 478K active corporations and ~20K CPAs serving them, average caseload is ~24 corporate clients per CPA — within plausible range for a full-time tax practitioner.
- **Accounting firms** (multi-seat) serving corporate clients: Based on SEC registration of accounting firms, the "Big 4 + mid-tier + small firms" landscape in the Philippines. Exact firm count not publicly available; estimated 2,000–5,000 registered accounting firms (ESTIMATED, based on PRC CPA in public practice sector and SEC firm registrations).

**Clients per professional per year:** 10–30 corporate ITR clients (for a CPA in a small-to-medium firm or solo practice); 50–200+ for large firms with dedicated corporate tax teams.

**B2B price per seat:** ₱999/month (Solo Pro tier, single CPA handling up to 30 corporate clients)

**Professional TAM:** 20,000 professionals × ₱999/month × 12 months = **₱239.8M/year ≈ ₱240M/year**

## Notes & Caveats

1. **PEZA/BOI exclusion**: An estimated 3,000–5,000 PEZA-registered export enterprises and a larger number of BOI-registered entities use special tax regimes (5% GIT under old PEZA rules; tiered ITH under CREATE). These are not addressable by a standard RCIT/MCIT engine — they require a separate incentive optimization tool. They should be excluded from the consumer TAM denominator.

2. **MCIT carryforward complexity**: The key professional value is not just current-year RCIT vs. MCIT comparison, but the 3-year carryforward of excess MCIT as a credit against RCIT. Many SME finance officers do not track carryforward MCIT credits, resulting in permanently lost tax savings. A tool that surfaces accumulated MCIT credits has immediate ROI for any corporation that was loss-making in a prior year.

3. **Small corporation rate (20%) identification**: Under CREATE, the reduced 20% rate requires active classification each year (net taxable income ≤ ₱5M AND total assets ≤ ₱100M as of year-end). A corporation that crossed either threshold mid-year pays the higher 25% rate. The tool needs to capture this conditional logic — not just a static flag.

4. **OPC growth**: One Person Corporations (OPC) grew from ~6,794 new registrations in 2023 to 8,640 in 2024 (+27%), suggesting a growing segment of solo entrepreneurs using corporate structure. OPCs face the same RCIT/MCIT obligation as regular stock corporations and are a natural early-adopter segment for a self-service corporate IT tool.

5. **Data gap**: No official BIR count of non-individual (corporate/partnership) registered taxpayers as of 2023 was publicly indexable. The BIR 2021 press release referenced 4,625,017 "business taxpayers" including individuals; a cleaner segmentation between corporate and individual business filers is in the BIR Annual Report PDF (not accessible via web search as of research date). The SEC active company count (~478K) is the best proxy for the corporate filer population.

6. **Consumer TAM vs. B2B TAM overlap**: There is an overlap risk — a corporation might pay the ₱199/month consumer tier while its CPA also uses a professional seat. In practice, the corporation would choose one or the other. The TAM calculation above double-counts slightly; in practice, roughly 70% of corporations would be served via the CPA's professional subscription rather than direct corporate subscription, reducing effective consumer TAM to ~30% of the ₱1.03B figure (≈₱309M) with the professional TAM remaining at ₱240M. Total de-duplicated TAM ≈ ₱549M.
