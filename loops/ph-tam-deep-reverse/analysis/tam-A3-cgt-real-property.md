## Consumer Segment

**Who:** Individual or corporate sellers/transferors of real property classified as capital assets — i.e., anyone who sells, exchanges, or otherwise disposes of residential, commercial, agricultural, or industrial land or improvements not used in trade or business. Must file BIR Form 1706 within 30 days of the transaction and pay 6% CGT on the gross selling price or zonal/fair market value, whichever is higher.

**Population:**

The BIR does not publish an annual count of Form 1706 returns filed. This figure must be derived from supply-side (new development) and secondary-market proxies:

- **123,985 residential units** covered under new License-to-Sell projects issued by DHSUD, January–November 2023 — Source: DHSUD press release / MB.com.ph, December 2023 — Confidence: OFFICIAL (partial year; annualized ≈ 135,000 new residential units per year)
- **116,427 residential building permits** issued in 2024 — Source: DHSUD 2024 Yearender Statistics — Confidence: OFFICIAL
  - These two figures bound the primary (new-development) residential market at approximately **120,000–135,000 new residential property sale events per year**
- **Secondary market residential** (resale of existing homes): Estimated **180,000–270,000 transactions per year** — Source: DERIVED from primary market estimate × 1.5–2.0 secondary-market multiplier (typical ratio in emerging property markets; no PH-specific secondary market count is publicly available) — Confidence: DERIVED
- **Commercial, industrial, agricultural land**: Estimated additional **50,000–100,000 ONETT transactions per year** — Source: ESTIMATED (no published count; accounts for land transfers, commercial property sales, agricultural lot transfers) — Confidence: ESTIMATED
- **Total estimated Form 1706 (CGT Real Property) transactions per year: ~350,000–500,000** — Confidence: DERIVED
  - Cross-check: PSA 2020 Census shows 9.76M households (45.2% of 21.58M owned/amortizing) acquired housing through purchase. If housing stock accumulates over ~25 years, implied annual purchase transaction rate ≈ 390,000/year — broadly consistent — Confidence: DERIVED

**Data gap:** BIR does not publicly release Form 1706 return count. The ~6M "ONETT taxpayers" figure cited in Statista/BIR appears to be a cumulative registered count (all years, all ONETT categories) rather than annual flow. Annual Form 1706 filers are estimated at 350,000–500,000.

**Addressable fraction:**
- Target consumer (property seller/buyer arranging their own CGT computation) is typically a property owner managing a one-time transaction; internet penetration among urban property-owning class (who engage in formal titled-property transfers): ~80%
- Not all sellers will use a digital tool; many delegate entirely to a real estate lawyer, CPA, or broker — estimated 40% of property sellers are "DIY-aware" or semi-DIY
- Combined addressable fraction: **80% × 40% = 32%** of total transaction population
- Use **30%** (round, conservative) for addressable consumer fraction

**Addressable consumer population:** 425,000 (midpoint) × 30% = **127,500** consumers per year

**Note:** Unlike a subscription product, CGT computation is a one-time annual event tied to a specific transaction. The practical monetization model is per-transaction (not monthly), with per-use pricing of ₱499–₱999. However, for comparison with other tools, we also show a monthly subscription equivalent.

**Current professional cost:**
- CPA preparation of ONETT computation sheet + assistance with BIR filing: ₱5,000–₱15,000 per transaction — Source: Various PH CPA/law firm rate sheets and community forum posts (loft.ph, PinoyExchange) — Confidence: ESTIMATED
- Real estate lawyer engagement for full title transfer coordination (includes CGT computation): ₱15,000–₱50,000 per transaction (all-in) — Source: Forum estimates and broker community posts — Confidence: ESTIMATED

**Our consumer price:** ₱499 per transaction (one-time CGT computation + compliance checklist) OR ₱199/month subscription

**Consumer TAM (per-transaction model):** 127,500 × ₱499 = **₱63.6M/year**
**Consumer TAM (subscription model):** 127,500 × ₱2,388/year = **₱304M/year** (but overstates: most consumers would use the tool once, not annually)

**Practical Consumer TAM:** Using the per-transaction model as primary: **₱63.6M/year**

## Notes & Caveats

1. **Critical data gap — Form 1706 annual count:** The BIR does not publicly publish the number of Form 1706 returns filed annually. The 350,000–500,000 estimate is derived from construction permit data + secondary market multiplier. An FOI request to the BIR's Statistics Division would clarify this. The BIR's own ~6M "ONETT taxpayers" figure appears to be a cumulative stock count across all ONETT types, not annual Flow.

2. **Per-transaction vs. subscription pricing:** CGT on real property is a one-time annual event (and most sellers sell once every 5–10 years). A monthly subscription creates a mismatch — most consumers would prefer per-transaction pricing (₱499–₱999). The professional tier is more naturally subscription-based (multiple transactions per year). The TAM table uses per-transaction pricing for consumer and monthly subscription for professional.

3. **eONETT transition:** BIR launched the eONETT System in 2023 (RMC 56-2023), enabling online filing of ONETT and issuance of eCAR. This digitalization increases tool relevance — as property sellers move online, a computation tool that outputs ONETT-ready numbers becomes directly valuable.

4. **CGT avoidance behavior:** Some sellers (particularly in provincial areas or informal market) under-declare selling prices to minimize CGT. A tool that clearly shows consequences and legal compliance path could serve as an education plus computation tool, increasing addressable market among previously non-compliant sellers.

5. **Professional TAM dominates:** The professional segment (₱228M) significantly exceeds consumer (₱64M). This is typical for property-sector compliance tools — professionals handle high transaction volumes and would pay for tools that reduce per-transaction computation time.
