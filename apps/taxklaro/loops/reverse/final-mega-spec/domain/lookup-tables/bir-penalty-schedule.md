# BIR Penalty Schedule — Philippine Freelance & Self-Employed Tax Optimizer

Complete reference of all BIR penalties applicable to self-employed individuals and freelancers. Used by the penalty-calculator module and the risk-exposure estimator.

**Legal basis:** NIRC Secs. 247–282 (Penalties), as amended by TRAIN (RA 10963) and EOPT Act (RA 11976)
**Administrative schedules:** RMO No. 7-2015 (Annex A), RMO No. 19-2007, RR No. 6-2024
**Last updated:** 2026-03-01

---

## Part 1: Civil Penalties (Non-Criminal)

These apply automatically upon assessment. No court action needed.

---

### 1.1 Surcharges (NIRC Section 248)

Surcharges are one-time percentage penalties added on top of the basic tax due.

| Violation | Large/Medium Taxpayer | Small/Micro Taxpayer (EOPT) | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Failure to file return on time | 25% of basic tax due | **10% of basic tax due** | Sec. 248(A)(1) |
| Failure to pay tax on time | 25% of amount unpaid | **10% of amount unpaid** | Sec. 248(A)(3) |
| Failure to pay deficiency tax within assessment deadline | 25% of deficiency | **10% of deficiency** | Sec. 248(A)(4) |
| Willful neglect to file (deliberate non-filing) | 50% of basic tax due | 50% (fraud rate; EOPT reduction does NOT apply) | Sec. 248(B) |
| False or fraudulent return willfully made | 50% of basic tax due | 50% (fraud rate; EOPT reduction does NOT apply) | Sec. 248(B) |
| Substantial underdeclaration (>30% of declared sales/income) | 50% of basic tax due | 50% (fraud rate; EOPT reduction does NOT apply) | Sec. 248(B) |
| Substantial overstatement of deductions (>30%) | 50% of basic tax due | 50% (fraud rate; EOPT reduction does NOT apply) | Sec. 248(B) |

**Important notes:**
- EOPT surcharge reduction (25% → 10%) applies ONLY to Small and Micro taxpayers (gross sales < ₱20M).
- The 50% fraud surcharge is NEVER reduced — it applies uniformly regardless of taxpayer tier.
- The "wrong venue" 25% surcharge (filing at incorrect RDO) was **ELIMINATED** by EOPT Act effective January 22, 2024.
- Surcharge is computed on the BASIC TAX DUE only, not on interest or compromise penalties.
- Surcharge is imposed ONCE per violation, not per day.

**EOPT taxpayer tiers (for surcharge and interest determination):**

| Tier | Annual Gross Sales | Surcharge Rate | Interest Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Micro | < ₱3,000,000 | 10% | 6% per annum |
| Small | ₱3,000,000 – < ₱20,000,000 | 10% | 6% per annum |
| Medium | ₱20,000,000 – < ₱1,000,000,000 | 25% | 12% per annum |
| Large | ₱1,000,000,000+ | 25% | 12% per annum |

---

### 1.2 Interest (NIRC Section 249)

Interest is computed daily on the unpaid tax from due date to full payment date.

| Period | Rate | Applicable Taxpayer | Formula |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-January 1, 2018 | 20% per annum | All | (Tax due × 20%) × (Days / 365) |
| January 1, 2018 – January 21, 2024 | 12% per annum | All | (Tax due × 12%) × (Days / 365) |
| January 22, 2024 onwards — Large/Medium | 12% per annum | Large/Medium | (Tax due × 12%) × (Days / 365) |
| January 22, 2024 onwards — Small/Micro | **6% per annum** | Small/Micro | (Tax due × 6%) × (Days / 365) |

**Notes:**
- Interest runs from the date the tax was DUE (not from assessment date) until full payment.
- Interest is computed on a daily basis (not monthly).
- Interest accrues on the BASIC TAX DUE. Surcharge and compromise penalties also accrue interest if unpaid beyond their own due dates.
- Deficiency interest: assessed in BIR assessment notices on top of basic tax.
- Delinquency interest: accrues after the deadline for paying an assessment has lapsed.

---

### 1.3 Section 250 — Failure to File Information Returns

Applies to: SAWT (Summary Alphalist of Withholding Tax), Quarterly Alphalist of Payees (QAP), Annual Alphalist of Employees/Payees, BIR Form 2307 (Certificate of Creditable Withholding Tax).

| Violation | Large/Medium Taxpayer | Small/Micro Taxpayer (EOPT) |
|---|---|---|
| Failure to file/submit information return | ₱1,000 per failure | **₱500 per failure** |
| Annual aggregate cap (same calendar year) | ₱25,000 | **₱12,500** |

**"Per failure" definition (per RMC 51-2009):** Failure to supply the required information for each buyer or seller of goods and services constitutes a SINGLE punishable act. Thus "per failure" = per required filing/submission, not per line item.

**Context for self-employed freelancers:** If the freelancer is the PAYEE (client withholds and issues 2307), the obligation for filing the 2307 and Alphalist lies with the PAYOR (client/withholding agent). The freelancer's obligation is to submit the SAWT attachment to their own returns.

## Part 2: Compromise Penalties (Administrative)

Compromise penalties are amounts the BIR offers taxpayers to settle criminal liability WITHOUT court prosecution. They are VOLUNTARY — the taxpayer must consent. If a taxpayer refuses to pay the suggested compromise, the BIR escalates to criminal prosecution.

**Key principle:** Compromise penalties are IN ADDITION TO (not a substitute for) civil penalties (surcharges + interest). A late-filed return with tax due incurs all three: surcharge + interest + compromise penalty.

**Legal basis:** NIRC Sec. 204(A); RMO No. 7-2015 (Annex A); RMO No. 19-2007

**Fraud violations CANNOT be compromised.** Violations involving fraud (Section 254) and subsequent/repeat violations are referred directly for criminal prosecution.

---

| Over ₱20,000 – ₱50,000 | ₱10,000 | ₱5,000 |

| Over ₱100,000 – ₱500,000 | ₱20,000 | ₱10,000 |

### 2.6 Compromise Penalty: Invoicing and Receipt Violations

Source: RMO No. 7-2015, Annex A; NIRC Sec. 264; criminal penalty: fine ₱1,000–₱50,000 + imprisonment 2–4 years

| Violation | 1st Offense | 2nd Offense | Subsequent Offenses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Failure to issue receipts/invoices | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise (criminal prosecution) |
| Refusal to issue receipts/invoices | ₱25,000 | ₱50,000 | Not subject to compromise |
| Issuing receipts not truly reflecting required information | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise |
| Duplicate copy blank but original detached and unaccounted | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise |
| Amount understated in taxpayer's copy vs. copy issued to buyer | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise |
| Use of unregistered/unauthorized receipts or invoices | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise |
| Possession or use of multiple/double receipts or invoices | ₱10,000 | ₱20,000 | Not subject to compromise |

**EOPT (RR 7-2024) update:** "Official Receipt" (OR) is no longer the primary sales document for SERVICES. Effective April 27, 2024, services must be covered by an INVOICE. Issuing an "Official Receipt" after December 31, 2024 for service transactions (where an Invoice is required) is considered tantamount to failure to issue an Invoice and incurs the penalties above.

**50% EOPT reduction for invoicing violations:** For violations of Sections 237 and 238 (invoicing/receipt issuance), micro and small taxpayers receive a **50% reduction** on the compromise penalty amount per RR 6-2024. Thus:
- Micro/Small, 1st offense (failure to issue): ₱10,000 × 50% = **₱5,000**
- Micro/Small, 2nd offense (failure to issue): ₱20,000 × 50% = **₱10,000**

**Criminal penalty (Section 264(a)) for failure/refusal to issue receipts:**
- Fine: ₱1,000 to ₱50,000
- Imprisonment: 2 to 4 years

**Criminal penalty (Section 264(b) / RR 13-2021) for unauthorized printing of receipts:**
- Fine: ₱500,000 to ₱10,000,000
- Imprisonment: 6 to 10 years
- Additional penalty: permanent closure if violations exceed 180 cumulative days within a taxable year

## Part 3: Criminal Penalties

Criminal prosecution requires willfulness or fraud. The BIR files cases with the Department of Justice (DOJ) via the Run After Tax Evaders (RATE) program. Criminal penalties are imposed by COURTS after conviction, not by BIR administrative assessment.

**Key principle:** Criminal penalties are ON TOP OF — not a substitute for — civil penalties (surcharges + interest + compromise). A convicted taxpayer pays all civil penalties PLUS the criminal fine.

**Fraud violations CANNOT be compromised** — they must go to criminal prosecution.

---

### 3.1 Section 255 — Failure to File, Supply Information, Pay Tax, Withhold, Remit

Covers: failure to file returns, failure to supply correct information, failure to pay tax, failure to withhold or remit taxes, failure to refund excess taxes withheld on compensation.

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not less than ₱10,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not less than 1 year but not more than 10 years |

**Triggers Section 255:**
- Willful failure to file required return
- Willful failure to pay tax due
- Deliberate failure to withhold or remit withheld taxes
- Failure to supply correct and accurate information (repeated, demonstrating willfulness)

**Note:** For non-willful, first-time late filing, BIR typically imposes compromise penalties (Part 2 above) rather than criminal prosecution.

---

### 3.2 Section 254 — Willful Attempt to Evade or Defeat Tax (Tax Evasion/Fraud)

Covers: intentional misrepresentation of income, fabricated expenses, falsified books, use of fake/falsified Official Receipts, Letters of Authority, CARs, Tax Credit Certificates.

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not less than ₱30,000 but not more than ₱100,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not less than 2 years but not more than 4 years |

**Triggers Section 254:**
- Knowingly filing a false or fraudulent return with intent to evade
- Using ghost receipts (fake Official Receipts from non-existent or unrelated entities)
- Deliberate understatement of income or overstatement of deductions
- Underdeclaration exceeding 30% of declared income (Section 248(B) — creates prima facie evidence of fraud)

**CANNOT be compromised.** Section 254 violations must proceed to criminal prosecution.

**Interaction with 10-year prescriptive period:** A finding of Section 254 fraud also triggers the extraordinary 10-year assessment period under Section 222(a), allowing the BIR to assess taxes for up to 10 years from date of discovery (vs. the ordinary 3-year period).

---

### 3.3 Section 264(a) — Failure to Issue Receipts/Invoices (Criminal Level)

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not less than ₱1,000 but not more than ₱50,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not less than 2 years but not more than 4 years |

This is the CRIMINAL level of the same violation covered by compromise penalties in Section 2.6. Criminal prosecution is pursued when the compromise is refused or for repeat/subsequent offenses.

---

### 3.4 Section 264(b) / RR 13-2021 — Unauthorized Printing of Receipts/Invoices

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not less than ₱500,000 but not more than ₱10,000,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not less than 6 years but not more than 10 years |
| Additional | Permanent closure if cumulative violations exceed 180 days in a taxable year |

Covers: printing ORs/invoices without BIR authority, double or multiple sets of ORs/invoices, printing of unnumbered receipts, printing fraudulent receipts.

---

### 3.5 Section 275 — Violation of Other Provisions (No Specific Penalty)

Catch-all for violations where no specific penalty is prescribed elsewhere.

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not more than ₱1,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not more than 6 months |
| Or both | Fine + imprisonment |

Applies to: failure to display COR (₱1,000 compromise), failure to keep books at place of business, failure to submit quarterly printer's report, many other minor administrative violations.

---

### 3.6 Section 258 — Failure to Register

| Element | Amount/Term |
|---|---|
| Fine | Not less than ₱5,000 but not more than ₱20,000 |
| Imprisonment | Not less than 6 months but not more than 2 years |

## Part 4: Underdeclaration — How It Escalates

This section explains the escalation chain for underdeclaration, which is particularly relevant for freelancers who may underreport income.

### 4.1 Below 30% Underdeclaration

- BIR may assess basic tax deficiency + 25% surcharge (10% for micro/small) + 12%/6% interest + compromise penalty (from table in Section 2.1).
- 3-year prescriptive period for assessment (Section 203 NIRC).
- Ordinary civil case; compromise possible.

### 4.2 Above 30% Underdeclaration (Substantial Underdeclaration)

**Trigger:** Failure to report sales/receipts/income in an amount EXCEEDING 30% of what was declared. OR claiming deductions exceeding 30% of actual allowable deductions.

- Constitutes PRIMA FACIE EVIDENCE of a false or fraudulent return (NIRC Section 248(B)).
- BIR imposes the **50% fraud surcharge** (instead of 25% or 10%).
- **Burden of proof shifts to taxpayer** to rebut the fraud presumption.
- Extraordinary **10-year prescriptive period** (Section 222(a)) may apply from date of discovery.
- BIR may recommend criminal prosecution under Section 254 (tax evasion).
- CANNOT be compromised.

### 4.3 Total Penalty Stack for Underdeclaration (Worked Example)

Scenario: Freelancer with ₱200,000 gross receipts declared, actual receipts ₱320,000. Underdeclaration: ₱120,000 = 60% of declared amount (>30% threshold).

| Component | Calculation | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Additional income tax due (graduated, net of deductions) | (assume) ₱30,000 | ₱30,000 |
| Fraud surcharge (50%) | ₱30,000 × 50% | ₱15,000 |
| Interest (12%, 2 years — medium/large) | ₱30,000 × 12% × 2 | ₱7,200 |
| Compromise penalty (₱20,001–₱50,000 bracket) | — | ₱10,000 |
| **Total additional liability** | | **₱62,200** |

If Micro/Small taxpayer: surcharge stays at 50% (fraud rate — EOPT reduction does NOT apply to fraud). Interest is 6%, not 12%.

## Part 5: Prescriptive Periods for Tax Assessment

### 5.1 Ordinary Assessment Period — Section 203

**Period:** 3 years
**Starts from:** The LATER of (a) the last day prescribed by law for filing the return, OR (b) the actual date the return was filed (if filed late).

| Scenario | Prescriptive Period Start |
|---|---|
| Return filed ON TIME | From the deadline for filing |
| Return filed LATE | From the actual date of late filing |
| Return NOT filed | Does not start (10-year period applies instead) |

**Collection after ordinary assessment:** BIR has 3 more years from date of assessment to collect by distraint, levy, or court proceeding.

### 5.2 Extraordinary Assessment Period — Section 222(a)

**Period:** 10 years
**Starts from:** Date of DISCOVERY of the false return, fraudulent return, or failure to file.
**Applies when:**
1. False return with INTENT to evade tax (deliberate error — must be proven by clear and convincing evidence, per Supreme Court en banc ruling in McDonald's Philippines Realty Corp. v. CIR, G.R. No. 247737)
2. Fraudulent return with intent to evade
3. Failure to file a return entirely (no return filed)

**Key Supreme Court ruling:** Unintentional or clerical errors in a return do NOT trigger the 10-year period. The BIR must prove the misstatement was INTENTIONAL AND DELIBERATE with clear and convincing evidence. Mere size of understatement alone is insufficient.

**Exception trigger:** Underdeclaration exceeding 30% creates prima facie fraud under Section 248(B), which effectively shifts the burden and supports application of the 10-year period.

**Collection after fraud assessment:** BIR has 5 years from date of assessment to collect (Section 222(c)).

### 5.3 Prescriptive Period Summary Table

| Scenario | Assessment Period | Collection Period | Total Exposure Window |
|---|---|---|---|
| Return filed on time, no fraud | 3 years from filing deadline | 3 years from assessment | ~6 years max |
| Return filed late, no fraud | 3 years from actual filing date | 3 years from assessment | ~6 years max |
| Return not filed | 10 years from discovery | 5 years from assessment | ~15 years max |
| False/fraudulent return (deliberate, proven) | 10 years from discovery | 5 years from assessment | ~15 years max |
| Underdeclaration >30% (prima facie fraud) | 10 years from discovery | 5 years from assessment | ~15 years max |

### 5.4 Waiver of Prescriptive Period

Taxpayers may voluntarily execute a "Waiver of the Defense of Prescription" to extend the BIR's assessment period. This is common during ongoing audit negotiations. A waiver:
- Is valid only if executed BEFORE the original prescriptive period lapses.
- Must follow strict BIR form requirements (per RMO 20-90 as updated).
- Extends the period for the duration specified in the waiver.

## Part 6: Tax Amnesty Programs

### 6.1 General Tax Amnesty — PROPOSED (NOT YET LAW as of March 2026)

**Status:** Senate Bill No. 60 and House Bill No. 2653 are PENDING in Congress. Not yet enacted.

**What is proposed:**
- One-time amnesty covering all national internal revenue taxes (income tax, withholding tax, VAT, percentage tax, etc.) for taxable years **2007 to 2024**.
- Amnesty rate: **2% of total assets** OR **5% of net worth** as of December 31, 2024 (whichever the taxpayer chooses).
- Immunity: full immunity from civil, criminal, and administrative cases for covered taxes.
- Confidentiality: amnesty returns cannot be used in investigations.
- BIR audit protection: books for covered years not subject to audit (except for refund/credit/incentive claims).
- Early payment incentive: additional 20% discount for early payment.

**Implementation guidance for the tool:** This is NOT yet a feature. Flag this as PENDING LEGISLATION. If enacted, it would significantly change the exposure calculation.

### 6.2 Estate Tax Amnesty (RA 11956) — CLOSED

**Status:** CLOSED. Final deadline was June 16, 2025.
**Scope:** Estates of decedents who died on or before May 31, 2022.
**Not applicable to self-employed income tax.**

### 6.3 Voluntary Assessment and Payment Program (VAPP)

**Status:** Periodic — check latest BIR issuances.
**Mechanism:** BIR periodically issues Revenue Regulations offering taxpayers to voluntarily disclose and pay deficiency taxes for prior years at reduced/zero surcharge rates. Not a formal amnesty but achieves similar result.
**Applicability:** Self-employed freelancers who discover unreported income from prior years can avail if a VAPP program is currently active.

### 6.4 Abatement of Penalties (NIRC Section 204(B))

**Status:** Available — ongoing BIR mechanism.
**Mechanism:** Taxpayer may apply for abatement (reduction or cancellation) of surcharges and interest if there is "reasonable cause" for non-compliance.
**Grounds for abatement:**
- Force majeure (typhoon, earthquake, etc.)
- BIR system errors (eFPS downtime, etc.)
- Good faith misapplication of tax law
- Honest mistakes based on BIR's own prior rulings
**How to avail:** File BIR Form 2105 with supporting documentation at the Revenue District Office.
**Not available for:** Fraud violations; willful neglect.

## Part 7: Practical Penalty Computation Logic

### 7.1 Three-Component Late Filing Penalty Formula

For a return filed late WITH tax due:

```
Total Penalties = Surcharge + Interest + Compromise Penalty

Where:
  Surcharge = Tax Due × Surcharge Rate (25% standard; 10% micro/small; 50% fraud)
  Interest = Tax Due × Interest Rate (12% standard; 6% micro/small) × (Days Late / 365)
  Compromise Penalty = Amount from Section 2.1 table based on Tax Due bracket
```

### 7.2 Example: Micro Taxpayer (Freelancer, gross sales ₱800K), 1701Q filed 60 days late

- Quarterly income tax due: ₱8,000
- Taxpayer tier: MICRO (gross sales < ₱3M)
- Late by: 60 days

```
Surcharge = ₱8,000 × 10% = ₱800
Interest = ₱8,000 × 6% × (60/365) = ₱79.12
Compromise Penalty = ₱3,000 (bracket: Over ₱5,000 – ₱10,000)
Total = ₱800 + ₱79 + ₱3,000 = ₱3,879
```

### 7.3 Example: Micro Taxpayer, 2551Q filed late with NO tax due (nil)

- Percentage tax due: ₱0 (no gross sales in quarter)
- Taxpayer: MICRO
- 1st offense

```
Surcharge = ₱0 (no tax due = no surcharge)
Interest = ₱0 (no tax due = no interest)
Compromise Penalty = ₱1,000 (nil return, 1st offense)
Total = ₱1,000
```

### 7.4 Tax Mapping / TCVD Discovery (New Unregistered Freelancer)

If discovered during BIR Tax Mapping or TCVD:

| Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Failure to register | ₱20,000 (city) / ₱10,000 (1st class municipality) / ₱5,000 (2nd class) / ₱2,000 (3rd class) — inclusive of all other violations found |
| Failure to display COR | ₱1,000 (but included in registration penalty bundle) |
| Failure to issue receipts/invoices | ₱10,000 1st offense (but may be included in registration bundle) |
| Back taxes (income + percentage tax for unreported years) | Computed separately: tax due + surcharge + interest + compromise per return |

**BIR Oplan Kandado:** Additionally, BIR can issue a closure order for 5+ days for failure to register or failure to issue receipts. Business cannot operate during closure.

## Appendix A: Common Penalty Computation Checklist for Freelancers

Use this checklist when computing total exposure for a self-employed individual:

1. **Determine taxpayer tier** (Micro / Small / Medium / Large) by prior year gross sales.
2. **Identify all unfiled returns** — list each form and period missed.
3. **For each late/unfiled return with tax due:** Apply surcharge (10% or 25%) + interest (6% or 12% daily, from due date) + compromise penalty from Table 2.1.
4. **For each late/unfiled nil return:** Apply compromise penalty (₱1,000 per offense, per offense sequence).
5. **For underdeclaration:** Determine if >30%. If yes, apply 50% surcharge (overrides EOPT reduction). Assess criminal exposure.
6. **Check information return obligations:** SAWT attached to 1701Q and 1701/1701A? If not, apply ₱1,000 or ₱500 per missed submission (within ₱25,000 or ₱12,500 annual cap).
7. **Check registration:** Is COR current? COR displayed? Invoices/ORs registered and issued?
8. **Apply prescriptive period filter:** Years older than 3 years (or 10 years for fraud/no-filing) are already prescribed and cannot be assessed.
9. **Sum all components** for total exposure.
10. **Flag general tax amnesty:** If SB 60 / HB 2653 is enacted before user takes action, exposure calculation changes significantly.
